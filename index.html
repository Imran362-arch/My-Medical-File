üìò Identifying Vertebral Spinous Processes

üìÑ Page 4-5

ü¶† Clinical Landmark Relationships

I. Key Vertebral Levels

  • C2: Most superior palpable bony protuberance below skull
  • C7: Prominent eminence at base of neck (vertebra prominens)
  • T1: Often more prominent than C7
  • T3: Level with root of scapular spine
  • T7: Level with inferior angle of scapula
  • T12: Midpoint between inferior scapular angle and iliac crest
  • L4: Level with highest point of iliac crest
  • S2: Level with sacral dimples (PSIS)

II. Important Anatomical Relationships

  • Ligamentum nuchae: Extends between C7 and external occipital protuberance
  • Thoracic spinous processes: Long and sharply sloped downward
  • Lumbar/sacral spinous processes: Shorter and less sloped
  • Tip of coccyx palpable between gluteal masses

üìò Spinal Cord and Subarachnoid Space

üìÑ Page 5-6

üîπ Concise Summary:

I. Anatomical Termination Points

Structure Normal Termination Level Clinical Significance
Spinal Cord L1-L2 disc level (range: T12 to L2-L3) Conus medullaris location
Subarachnoid Space S2 level Safe site for lumbar puncture
Cauda Equina Below L1-L2 level Lumbar and sacral nerve roots

II. Lumbar Puncture Considerations

  • Subarachnoid space can be safely accessed in lower lumbar region
  • L4 spinous process identified at level of iliac crests
  • Safe puncture sites: Between L3-L4 or L4-L5
  • Fetal position opens spaces between vertebral arches
  • Avoids risk to spinal cord

I. Cauda Equina Syndrome

üí° Case 1: Cauda Equina Syndrome

Presentation: 50-year-old male with severe lower back pain, fecal incontinence, urinary retention, leg numbness/weakness

Findings: Reduced knee extension (L4), reduced foot dorsiflexion (L5), reduced ankle reflexes (S1-S2), perineal numbness (S3-S5), reduced anal tone

Cause: L2-L3 disc herniation compressing cauda equina

Management: Surgical emergency requiring decompression

II. Cervical Spinal Cord Injury

üí° Case 2: Cervical Spinal Cord Injury

Presentation: 45-year-old male post-MVA with cervical injury, respiratory arrest

Anatomy: Phrenic nerve (C3-C5) for diaphragm. Injury above C5 affects breathing

Findings: Quadriplegia, sensory loss in all limbs

Significance: Lesion at or above C5 affects brachial plexus function

üí° Clinical Pearl: Lumbar Puncture Landmarks

The L4 spinous process, identified at the level of the iliac crests, serves as the key landmark for safe lumbar puncture. The subarachnoid space between L3-L4 or L4-L5 can be accessed without risk to the spinal cord, which typically terminates at L1-L2. This anatomical relationship is crucial for procedures like spinal anesthesia and cerebrospinal fluid sampling.

Key Management Principle: Back surface anatomy provides essential landmarks for clinical procedures, neurological assessment, and diagnosis of spinal conditions. Understanding the relationships between palpable bony landmarks and underlying structures enables accurate localization of vertebral levels, safe performance of procedures like lumbar puncture, and early recognition of serious conditions like cauda equina syndrome. The combination of anatomical knowledge and careful physical examination remains fundamental to effective back assessment.
