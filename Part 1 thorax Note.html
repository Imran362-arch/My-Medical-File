ðŸ”¹ General Description:

I. Thoracic Cavity Structure

  • Thorax: Irregularly shaped cylinder with narrow superior opening and large inferior opening
  • Superior thoracic aperture: Open, continuous with neck
  • Inferior thoracic aperture: Closed by diaphragm
  • Musculoskeletal wall: Flexible, segmentally arranged vertebrae, ribs, muscles, and sternum

II. Thoracic Cavity Subdivisions

  • Left and right pleural cavities: Each surrounding a lung
  • Mediastinum: Central partition containing heart, esophagus, trachea, major nerves, and blood vessels

ðŸ“˜ Thoracic Functions

ðŸ“„ Page 126

ðŸ”¹ Primary Functions:

I. Breathing

  • Contains lungs and breathing machinery (diaphragm, thoracic wall, ribs)
  • Volume changes via diaphragm movement and rib cage expansion

II. Protection of Vital Organs

  • Houses and protects heart, lungs, and great vessels
  • Diaphragm dome shape provides protection to some abdominal organs
  • Liver under right dome; stomach and spleen under left dome
  • Superior poles of kidneys lie on diaphragm

III. Conduit Function

  • Mediastinum acts as passage for structures between body regions
  • Esophagus, vagus nerves, thoracic duct pass through mediastinum
  • Phrenic nerves pass through to supply diaphragm
  • Trachea, thoracic aorta, superior vena cava course within mediastinum

ðŸ”¹ Superior Thoracic Aperture

  • Borders: Body of vertebra TI posteriorly, medial margin of rib I on each side, manubrium anteriorly
  • Oblique angle, facing somewhat anteriorly
  • Pleural cavities extend above rib I into neck root
  • Structures pass between neck/head and thorax vertically

ðŸ”¹ Inferior Thoracic Aperture

  • Borders: Body of TXII, rib XII, distal end of rib XI, costal margin, xiphoid process
  • Large and expandable
  • Closed by diaphragm
  • Structures pass between abdomen and thorax through or posterior to diaphragm

ðŸ“˜ Relationship to Other Regions

ðŸ“„ Pages 132-133

ðŸ”¹ Regional Connections:

I. Neck

  • Superior thoracic aperture opens directly into neck root
  • Pleural cavities extend 2-3 cm above rib I into neck
  • Major visceral structures pass between neck and superior mediastinum

II. Upper Limb

  • Axillary inlet: Gateway to upper limb on each side of superior thoracic aperture
  • Formed by: Superior margin of scapula, clavicle, lateral margin of rib I
  • Large blood vessels pass over rib I between axillary inlet and superior thoracic aperture

III. Abdomen

  • Diaphragm separates thorax from abdomen
  • Structures pass between thorax and abdomen through or posterior to diaphragm
  • IVC: Pierces central tendon at TVIII
  • Esophagus: Penetrates muscular part at TX
  • Aorta: Passes posteriorly at TXII

ðŸ“„ Pages 133, 140-142

ðŸ”¹ Breast Structure:

I. Composition

  • Mammary glands, superficial fascia, and overlying skin
  • Located in pectoral region on anterior thoracic wall
  • Modified sweat glands in superficial fascia anterior to pectoral muscles

II. Internal Structure

  • Ducts and secretory lobules â†’ 15-20 lactiferous ducts â†’ nipple
  • Areola: Circular pigmented area around nipple
  • Suspensory ligaments: Support breast, continuous with dermis
  • Retromammary space: Loose connective tissue allowing movement

III. Vascular Supply & Innervation

  • Arterial: Axillary artery branches, internal thoracic artery, intercostal arteries
  • Venous: Parallels arteries â†’ axillary, internal thoracic, intercostal veins
  • Innervation: Anterior and lateral cutaneous branches of T2-T6 intercostal nerves
  • Lymphatic: 75% to axillary nodes, remainder to parasternal nodes

ðŸ’¡ Clinical Correlation: Breast Cancer

Breast cancer develops in acini, lactiferous ducts, and lobules. Tumor spread depends on cellular origin and affects treatment response. Lymphatic spread is complex - to axillary, supraclavicular, parasternal nodes, and even abdominal nodes or opposite breast.

Signs: Peau d'orange (orange peel texture), cancer en cuirasse (hard, woody skin)

Mastectomy risks: Damage to long thoracic nerve (winged scapula), nerve to latissimus dorsi

ðŸ“˜ Key Thoracic Features

ðŸ“„ Pages 134-139

ðŸ”¹ Vertebral Level TIV/V:

The horizontal plane through TIV/V disc is one of the most significant in the body because it:

  • Passes through sternal angle anteriorly
  • Separates superior from inferior mediastinum
  • Marks superior limit of pericardium
  • Marks where aortic arch begins and ends
  • Level where SVC penetrates pericardium
  • Level of tracheal bifurcation
  • Superior limit of pulmonary trunk

ðŸ”¹ Venous Shunts from Left to Right:

  • Right atrium receives deoxygenated blood on right side of midline
  • Blood from left side must cross midline to reach right side
  • Left brachiocephalic vein: Crosses posterior to manubrium
  • Hemiazygos/accessory hemiazygos veins: Drain left thoracic wall â†’ azygos vein â†’ SVC

ðŸ”¹ Segmental Neurovascular Supply:

  • Arteries from thoracic aorta and internal thoracic arteries
  • Posterior and anterior intercostal vessels run along inferior rib margins
  • Intercostal nerves (anterior rami of thoracic spinal nerves) run with vessels
  • Neurovascular bundle position important for clinical procedures

ðŸ”¹ Sympathetic System:

  • Preganglionic fibers from T1 to L2 spinal nerves
  • Sympathetic trunks: Paravertebral ganglia connected to spinal nerves
  • Fibers for head emerge from T1

ðŸ”¹ Flexible Wall & Breathing:

  • Rib posterior attachment superior to anterior attachment
  • Rib elevation moves anterior wall forward
  • Middle rib region inferior to ends â†’ lateral expansion when elevated
  • Diaphragm changes vertical dimension
  • Combined movements essential for breathing

ðŸ”¹ Diaphragm Innervation:

  • Phrenic nerves from cervical plexus (C3-C5, mainly C4)
  • Pass vertically through neck, superior thoracic aperture, mediastinum
  • Supply entire diaphragm including crura
  • Embryological origin explains cervical innervation
  • Spinal injuries below C5 do not affect diaphragm

Key Learning Points: The thorax is a complex cylindrical structure with vital respiratory, protective, and conduit functions. Understanding thoracic apertures, pleural cavities, mediastinal divisions, and neurovascular patterns is essential for clinical practice. The diaphragm's central role in respiration and its unique cervical innervation are particularly important concepts.
